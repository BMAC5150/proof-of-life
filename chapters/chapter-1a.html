Chapter 1A

At 9:08AM the executive director prepared the branch for a 9:30AM opening the way she had opened it for nine years.

Key in the lock. Lights. The furnace clicking on in the ceiling above the lobby.

The Hudson River Community Federal Credit Union. Croton-on-Hudson.

Twelve hundred square feet between a hardware store and a pizza place. One branch. Six employees. 4,200 members. A brass plaque by the door that said Chartered 1974.

She set her bag behind the counter. Logged into the core system.

The status light on the dashboard was red.

It had been green every morning for nine years. She had stopped noticing it years ago because it never changed.

Until now.

Member database. Zero records.

Accounts summary. Zero.

Transaction ledger. Zero.

She logged out. Logged back in. Same.

The cursor blinked against nothing.

She called the core system vendor.

Twenty minutes on hold. A technician. Diagnostics she had already run. Escalation. Another hold. A supervisor who asked about the backup.

Cloud-replicated, she said. Same vendor.

The supervisor went quiet.

She did not open the branch at 9:30AM.

She taped a handwritten notice to the glass door and called the Board chair.

System outage. Under investigation. Accounts federally insured. Working to restore. At her desk the phone was already ringing. Emails were pinging.

One. Then three. Then accelerating.

A man whose direct deposit had not arrived. A woman whose debit card was declined at the gas station. A member locked out of online banking since six in the morning. A small business owner whose payroll was supposed to clear and had not. An elderly woman whose pharmacy rejected her pre-authorized payment for insulin wanted to know why their system did not recognize her name.

System outage. Under investigation. Accounts federally insured. Working to restore.

She said it eleven times. She typed it even more.

The elderly woman said she was going to call Channel 12.

She stopped answering after that. The words had stopped meaning anything. The people at the other end could sense it.

The two tellers sat in the break room with coffee. The coffee was fine. Everything else was not.

She called the vendor again.

The vendor was not taking calls.

The furnace hummed in the ceiling. Warm air through the vents into an empty welcome area. Doing its job. Indifferent to the rest.

She sat at her desk in the closed branch.

Red light on the dashboard. Handwritten sign on the glass. 4,200 members who did not exist in a system that was supposed to know who they were. She did not know what to do.

The light on her dashboard stayed red.

It did not know how to do anything else.
