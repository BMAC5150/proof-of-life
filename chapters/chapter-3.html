Chapter Three

It was still dark when he crossed Karim Khan, though the darkness had thinned enough for the city to begin showing its injuries.

Tehran after a night like that did not look conquered. It looked used.

Beaten down and still moving. As if violence here had become less an event than a weather pattern. Something that rolled through, did its damage, then lingered in the air long after the noise was gone.

The young man walked fast with his backpack slung over one shoulder and one hand in the pocket of his jacket.

From a distance he could have been in any city on earth. Black sneakers. Loose dark pants. Faded olive coat. Hood pulled low. Head down. Phone in hand. A shape from an anonymous generation. Permanent exhaustion around the eyes. One more young person built for screens and deadlines and a future that kept failing to arrive.

Only here the sidewalks still held shattered glass.

Two burned cars sat nose to nose at an intersection as if they had crawled toward each other for warmth and died there. One was missing its windows. The other had lost its paint and now wore the dull peeled look of cooked metal. Farther down the block, black streaks ran up the wall of a pharmacy. A traffic light blinked uselessly over an empty crosswalk.

Somewhere off to the east came the crack of gunfire, flattened by distance into something almost casual. Not a battle. More like punctuation. Then a run of pops that might have been fireworks if you wanted to lie to yourself. In Tehran now people had become good at sorting sounds without ever admitting what they meant.

The air carried the residue of the night. Burned rubber. Diesel. Smoke. Cordite. The hot mineral smell of scorched metal. It sat at the back of the throat and stayed there.

He stepped around a line of dark bags laid against the curb under gray army blankets. No one stood with them. No one cried over them. Dawn had not yet reached the hour for grief. There were municipal men farther up the street hosing blood into the gutter with the detached efficiency of people assigned to a recurring task.

He looked once, then away.

He was not numb. That would have implied some final stage of moral damage, some dramatic deadness. He was not dead inside. He was simply moving inside a city where horror had become friction. Another obstacle between here and where he had to be.

Above him, mounted speakers crackled to life on a concrete utility pole and a male voice, flat and overamplified, spilled into the street.

Citizens are instructed to avoid unlawful gatherings. Curfew restrictions remain in force in designated areas. Cooperation with security forces is your patriotic duty.

Then, without pause, a verse from the Qur’an. Then a warning about sedition. Then patriotic music, thin and metallic through the public address horns.

He kept walking.

His phone showed the time, the battery level, and nothing else that mattered. No messages. No signal beyond the local carrier tag. No internet. The state had shut that down again overnight, which meant the entire city had been folded inward. Millions of people sealed inside their own borders, able to call, sometimes, but not to reach beyond the wall that had been dropped over them.

He slipped the phone into his coat pocket and adjusted the backpack.

Inside were a thermos of tea wrapped in an old towel, a laptop in a padded sleeve, a charger, a cheap notebook, a copy of the Qur’an his mother had given him when he was fourteen, and a protein bar that had been in there long enough to qualify as historical material. It was the kind of bag a student might carry. Or a junior programmer. Or a son going to visit family. It did not belong in a city that smelled like a firing line.

He passed the bakery where he used to buy sesame bread on Fridays. The glass was gone. The awning had burned away. The metal racks inside stood blackened and empty in the half-light. Someone had spray-painted over the soot in red. A slogan. He did not stop long enough to read it.

At the next corner he looked toward the schoolyard where boys used to play football in the late afternoon under cheap floodlights. The chain-link fence was still there. So were the lights. But the field itself had been covered with white sheets weighted at the corners by chunks of brick. Men moved quietly among them with clipboards and surgical masks. A temporary morgue. Or maybe not so temporary anymore.

The city was trying to wake up around the damage. That was the strangest part. Shutters rattling open on the blocks that had been spared. A woman sweeping broken glass from in front of a beauty supply shop. A boy on a motorbike weaving around debris with a plastic crate of flatbread tied to the back. A man dragging a hose across the sidewalk to wash down the front of a hardware store as if he could scrub the night off the neighborhood before customers arrived.

Tehran did not pause because people had died. It adjusted. Bent around the dead and kept going.

He turned toward Vali-e Asr and found what he already knew he would find. No buses. No trams. No metro entrance open. The transit stops held knots of stranded people looking down the road as if disappointment might eventually arrive in uniform and explain itself.

He checked the time again.

Too late.

A pressure began to build low in his chest, not dramatic at first. Just the tightening of numbers. Distance. Delay. The consequences of being late. The faces waiting for him at the other end of the drive. The questions. The reports. The look from the section chief that was somehow worse than shouting.

He could not show up late. Not there. Not now.

A taxi rolled slowly through the intersection, yellow body streaked with dust, one headlamp out. He stepped off the curb and raised a hand. The driver saw him and kept going.

The young man moved farther into the street.

This time the taxi stopped.

The driver leaned across the passenger seat and lowered the window two inches. He was maybe fifty. Heavy eyebrows, day-old beard, cigarette behind one ear. A man already tired of the morning.

“Where?”

The young man named a point outside the city.

The driver barked a laugh. “Public roads are blocked. No buses. No fuel coming in. Go ask the government.”

He almost said, I work for the government. He stared silently at the driver.

Instead he opened his coat, took out a card from the inside pocket, and held it up in the weak blue streetlight.

The driver’s face changed at once. Not dramatically. Just enough. The mouth tightened. The eyes flicked from the card to the young man’s face and then away again.

It was not exactly fear. It was calculation shaped by long habit. Fear after it had been normalized and filed down into obedience.

The card identified him as attached to a special technical unit under the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. Not infantry. Not field security. Something quieter. More ambiguous. The kind of branch ordinary people did not fully understand and had no wish to understand better.

The young man hated the card. Hated what it did to people’s posture. Hated the way doors opened or voices flattened when it appeared. Hated the parts of himself that had learned to use it anyway.

“Please,” he said, and heard how false that sounded after the card was already in view.

The driver unlocked the doors.

He got in.

The cab smelled of stale tobacco, vinyl, and the faint medicinal scent of wintergreen gum. The driver pulled away without another word.

For a few minutes they moved through the lower city in silence. Burned barricades. Charred dumpsters. Posters of martyrs and clerics pasted over older posters advertising mobile plans and skin cream and exam prep courses.

The young man set the backpack between his shoes and looked out the window.

He saw his own reflection layered over the city.

Pale face. Unshaven jaw. A look now worn by young men in every country with a data center and a rent crisis. Sleepless. Guarded. Slightly dislocated from the physical world.

He thought of the others already waiting at the site.

They would have made the same journey in different ways. Borrowed rides. Military transport. Shared cabs. Long walks. Each carrying some version of the same private life in a bag. Tea. Headphones. Family photos. Religious books. Energy drinks. Cheap cologne. Flash drives. Pills for stomach acid and panic.

People liked to imagine institutions as monoliths. Especially institutions like theirs. Disciplined. Ideological. Smooth.

The taxi merged onto the highway heading west.

Here the city spread and flattened. Apartment blocks gave way to industrial strips, then warehouse lots, then factories behind dirty walls. Billboards rose over the road in giant stages of contradiction. A perfume advertisement with a woman’s face mostly scratched away. A martyr poster. A state slogan about sacrifice. An ad for a telecom provider whose service no one trusted. A smiling family promoting domestic manufacturing under a sky too blue to have been real even before the smoke.

The farther they drove, the less visible the uprising became. Not because it was absent, but because distance made everything look administrative. Orderly. Managed. Fires reduced to stains. Rage reduced to checkpoints and roadblocks and repaired lanes.

The driver spoke for the first time in ten minutes.

“Long night.”

The young man looked at him.

The driver kept his eyes on the road. “My son is twenty-one. He was out there somewhere.” He nodded back toward the city. “Maybe still is.”

The young man said nothing.

After a moment the driver added, “You people should figure out what you’re doing to this country.”

Not angry. Not loud. Just tired.

The young man almost said, You people. As if he were not the same age as the son. As if he had not grown up under the same sermons, the same shortages, the same stale lies about sacrifice and destiny. As if he were not also carrying a bag and a laptop through the wreckage of somebody else’s decisions.

But the card in his pocket had already made argument impossible.

He turned back to the window.

Thirty kilometers outside Tehran, the taxi left the highway and rolled onto a gravel road edged by dead winter grass and rusted fencing. There was an abandoned gatehouse at the entrance, windows blown out, one side collapsed inward. The gate beside it stood open, chained back years ago and never repaired.

Beyond it sat a cluster of low buildings scattered without logic across a flat stretch of dirt. Quonset huts, utility sheds, prefab offices painted the same color as dust. Nothing about the place suggested importance. That was the point. It looked like somewhere agricultural records might be stored. Or irrigation maps. Or spare parts for tractors nobody used anymore.

The taxi slowed near the only structure that mattered.

From the outside it was just a reinforced concrete mound with a steel door set into one side, half-buried in packed earth. No sign. No visible antenna array. No flags. No armed guards in sight. The regime had many styles of power. This was one of its favorite kinds. Ugly, hidden, and expensive.

The young man handed the driver folded bills from an emergency stash he kept inside the laptop sleeve.

The driver hesitated, then took them.

For a moment neither man moved.

Then the young man got out, slung the backpack over his shoulder, and closed the door without thanking him.

He felt bad about that immediately and kept walking anyway.

The gravel crunched under his shoes. Wind pushed dust along the base of the bunker wall. Somewhere nearby a generator thudded with dull mechanical patience.

At the steel door he punched in the access code from memory. The keypad’s numbers were worn shiny by years of other hands doing the same thing. For one second nothing happened. Then the small indicator light shifted from red to green and the latch released with a heavy metallic click.

He pulled the door shut behind him and stood for a moment in the corridor. The latch clicked. The noise of the city was gone as if it had never existed. He adjusted the backpack on his shoulder and walked toward the voices.

The smell hit him first, as it always did.

Humidity from overworked cooling systems. Dust. Warm plastic. Heated wiring. Sweat soaked into fabric partitions and office chairs. Cheap detergent. Reheated rice. Saffron. Turmeric. Cardamom. Someone had already opened food from home. Someone always had.

The smell of men and women who spent too much time underground trying to bend invisible systems to the will of visible power.

Fluorescent light spilled down the narrow hall. The concrete walls sweated faintly. Somewhere deeper inside he could hear the layered murmur of voices, keyboard chatter, a printer spitting pages, the low constant rush of conditioned air, and one burst of laughter cut short almost as soon as it began.

Outside, the capital had staggered through another night of gunfire, slogans, bodies, and smoke.

Down here, nothing had changed.
